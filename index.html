What the override vote means for the median Marblehead tax bill

Marblehead voters are not just deciding whether they can absorb a bigger tax bill next year. They are deciding whether to give the town a larger and permanent claim on their property taxes, one that becomes part of the levy limit and then keeps growing under Proposition 2 1/2. For the homeowner in the middle of town, the baseline is a median single-family home assessed at $998,550 with a current tax bill of $8,548.

That median sits at the middle of Marblehead's 6,244 single-family homes, with 3,122 assessed higher and 3,122 lower. The town's published projections use the average single-family assessment of $1,291,507, a figure lifted by higher-value homes and about 29% above the median, which is why this page centers the more representative middle household instead.

What stands out for the median homeowner: Even without an override, Marblehead's median single-family tax bill has climbed about 22% over the past six years, roughly $1,540 on today's median home. Under the biggest combined option, Tier 3 plus trash, the median bill rises by $1,782 by Year 3, meaning the largest override package adds more in three years than the tax bill has risen over the previous six.
Why the median is the honest baseline: In a wealthy community, averages can blur more than they clarify. Marblehead's highest-value homes pull the average assessment upward, leaving it $292,957 higher than the median. For a kitchen-table understanding of what these scenarios mean for a typical homeowner, the middle single-family home is the fairer reference point.
Today's bill of $8,548 reflects the fiscal 2026 residential rate of $8.56 per $1,000 of assessed value applied to the median single-family assessment of $998,550. Scenario amounts use the town's April 8, 2026 override presentation: Tier 1 at $9 million, Tier 2 at $12 million, Tier 3 at $15 million, plus a separate trash override of $2,298,575 that would eliminate the roughly $262 annual household fee if it passes. Year 1 shows the first-year impact, Year 3 shows the fully phased-in totals, and Years 5 and 10 show the continuing effect once that added levy authority keeps compounding under Proposition 2 1/2. Actual bills still vary by individual assessment.
